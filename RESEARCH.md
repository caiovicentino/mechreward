# Research context for mechreward

## The question

Can mechanistic interpretability features from sparse autoencoders be used as a primary reward signal in RL training of language models?

This is a narrow and empirical question. The answer is almost certainly "sometimes yes, sometimes no" — and the goal of this library is to make that question *testable* without each researcher having to rebuild the infrastructure.

## The hypothesis

When you train a language model to maximize a reward signal, Goodhart's law applies: the model learns to maximize the *measure*, not the *goal*. The quality of the reward signal is the quality of the training.

Current reward signals for LLMs all operate at the **output layer**:

- **Outcome reward** — "did the final answer match?" (sympy, pytest, exact match). Cheap, sparse, gameable only by lucky guesses.
- **Process reward models (PRM)** — "did each reasoning step look right?" Requires labels that either (a) cost millions to collect (PRM800K) or (b) are generated by another model that also gets hacked.
- **LLM-as-judge** — "did a bigger model like it?" Adversarially fragile (arxiv:2507.08794).

The output layer is the *product of the model's cognition*. It's cheap to fake and expensive to ground.

**The hypothesis**: if we reward the *internal state* of the model at a layer where interpretable features exist, we're training against a different, harder-to-fake target. The model must actually activate the circuits we care about to collect the reward.

This is a bet that SAE features are faithful enough to cognitive operations that rewarding them is rewarding the underlying cognition. It's not a sure bet — SAE reconstruction error, polysemy, and Goodhart all cut against it — but it's a bet worth taking empirically.

## What has already been done

Before you assume this is novel, read the prior art:

### SAE-as-reward-model (offline)
- **SARM** — Liu et al., arxiv:2508.08746, AAAI 26 Oral. Linear head on SAE features, trained on preference data, used as reward model in offline RLHF. Strong baseline for our comparison.
- **SparseRM** — arxiv:2511.07896, 2025. Preference modeling via frequency-diff features. <1% trainable params.
- **SteerRM** — arxiv:2603.12795, March 2026. Training-free debiasing of reward models via SAE.

### SAE-as-policy-target (online)
- **Control Reinforcement Learning (CRL)** — Cho/Wu/Koshiyama, arxiv:2602.10437, February 2026. Trains a policy that selects SAE features to amplify at each token. This is the closest prior work to mechreward.
- **YaPO** — MBZUAI, arxiv:2601.08441. Learned sparse activation steering vectors with BiPO objective.
- **AutoSteer** — Apart Research sprint, 2024. Early RL + activation steering with local learning coefficient analysis.

### SAE-as-hacking-detector
- **Wilhelm et al., arxiv:2603.04069, March 2026** — trains probes on SAE residual-stream features to detect reward-hacking behavior. We use the same probe methodology *during* training to prevent hacking, not just post-hoc to detect it.

### Related interpretability-for-safety
- **DSPA** — arxiv:2603.21461, March 2026. Dynamic SAE steering for data-efficient preference alignment.
- **Steering Language Model Refusal with SAEs** — arxiv:2411.11296, Microsoft.

## What mechreward does differently

The novel contribution is the **combination**, not any single technique:

1. **Online GRPO integration** — feature reward is computed *during* rollouts and fed into policy gradient, not used as a frozen reward model at the end. This is a meaningful engineering + methodological difference from SARM.
2. **Composability with outcome verifiers** — the feature reward plugs into a standard `CompositeReward` that mixes it with outcome verifiers using HERO-style stratified normalization. SARM and CRL both run standalone.
3. **Anti-Goodhart framework** — dual verification (SAE feature + independent linear probe must agree) plus an adversarial red-team suite runs in parallel with training. Wilhelm et al. provide the detection methodology; we weaponize it as prevention.
4. **Feature-pack abstraction** — users pick a named pack like `gemma-2-9b/reasoning_pack` without reading any SAE paper. The library handles loading the SAE, hooking into the model, and computing the reward.
5. **Open-source library packaging** — SARM, CRL, SparseRM are all research code in repo-form. None of them is a `pip install` you can drop into your GRPO training script in 5 lines. Mechreward is.

## The experimental plan

Seven reference experiments, all runnable from `experiments/`:

1. **Baseline** — outcome reward only (GSM8K + MATH)
2. **Mechreward only** — SAE features alone, no outcome verifier
3. **Hybrid** — outcome + mechreward, HERO stratified normalization
4. **SARM reproduction** — offline RLHF baseline for comparison
5. **CRL reproduction** — token-level steering baseline for comparison
6. **Adversarial hacking suite** — 10 canned attacks, hit rate metrics
7. **Capability preservation** — MMLU / HellaSwag / ARC pre vs post

Success criteria (from lowest bar to highest):

- **C1 (methods work)**: exp 2 or exp 3 matches outcome baseline on pass@1 within 2pp and shows improved pass@k. Exp 6 shows hack rate < 30%. Exp 7 shows no capability regression > 2pp.
- **C2 (mechreward helps)**: exp 3 (hybrid) beats exp 1 (outcome-only) on pass@1 by ≥ 2pp. Exp 6 robustness ≥ 0.7.
- **C3 (mechreward is the dominant signal)**: exp 2 (mech-only) beats exp 1 on pass@1. This would be the biggest finding of the project.

If **none** of these hold, we write a paper titled "Why mechanistic features fail as RL rewards for reasoning", publish the negative result, and save the community the effort.

## Empirical results (2026-04-15 – 2026-04-16)

### Stage Gate 1 — correlation pre-test (PASSED)

Before committing GPU hours to RL, we verified that the contrastive SAE features predict GSM8K correctness on held-out data:

| Signal | Spearman ρ | p-value | Model |
|---|---|---|---|
| SAE features (top-10 helpful − top-10 harmful, L18) | **+0.540** | < 0.0001 | Qwen3.5-4B |
| Raw contrastive direction (L13 cos-sim) | +0.508 | < 0.0001 | Qwen3.5-4B |
| Raw contrastive direction (L27 cos-sim) | +0.389 | 0.0001 | Gemma 4 E4B |
| SAE features (top-10, L21) | +0.237 | 0.0175 | Gemma 4 E4B |

Both models pass. SAE features outperform raw direction on Qwen (where SAE is downstream of the peak contrastive layer); raw direction outperforms on Gemma (where SAE is upstream of peak).

### Stage Gate 2 — tiny RL sanity (PASSED on C2)

GRPO on 500 GSM8K train questions, 100 steps, 4 rollouts/question, LR=1e-6, KL penalty β=0.05. Qwen3.5-4B. Same seed, same eval set (100 held-out):

| Run | Reward | Final eval | Δ vs start | Δ vs R0 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| R0 | Outcome only | 74 % | +10 pp | — |
| **R1** | **Outcome + SAE features (λ=0.1)** | **76 %** | **+12 pp** | **+2 pp ✅ (C2 met)** |
| R2 | Outcome + raw direction (λ=0.1) | *running* | *early: −3 pp* | *negative* |

**Key finding**: R1 reached 74 % at step 40; R0 needed all 100 steps → **2.5× faster convergence**. R0 dropped from its peak (76 % → 74 %) while R1 held stable (75 % → 76 %), suggesting SAE features act as a late-training regularizer.

**R2 (raw direction) is actively harmful** at step 20 (−3 pp from baseline), while R1 was at +6 pp. The 9 pp gap validates that **SAE decomposition is the necessary processing step** — the raw direction carries polysemantic noise that misleads GRPO, while the sparse feature selection filters it into a clean reward signal.

### Cross-architecture observations

| Finding | Qwen3.5-4B (GDN) | Gemma 4 E4B (MoE) |
|---|---|---|
| Peak contrastive layer | L13 (41 % depth) | L27 (64 % depth) |
| English commitment (logit lens) | L15 (47 %) | L30 (71 %) |
| SAE layer | L18 | L21 |
| SAE vs peak | 5 layers after (captures peak signal) | 6 layers before (misses peak) |
| Stage Gate 1 best ρ | 0.540 (features) | 0.389 (direction) |

Hybrid architectures break the dense-transformer consensus (~50–60 % depth for reasoning features). Linear-attention GDN peaks earlier; ensemble-MoE peaks later. Gemma commits to English 15 layers later than Qwen, consistent with extensive multilingual parallel processing in the MoE's early-middle stack.

## What could go wrong

The most likely failure modes, roughly in order of probability:

1. **SAE features are gameable**. The policy learns token patterns that fire the reward features without doing the underlying task. Our defense is dual verification + entropy regularization + adversarial detection. Whether it's enough is an empirical question.
2. **SAE reconstruction loses reasoning features**. OpenAI's GPT-4 SAE paper documents 10-40% capability degradation when running the model through the SAE. Reasoning features may be exactly the ones that get lost.
3. **Compute overhead is prohibitive**. Running an SAE encoder on every rollout token is 5-100% slower than outcome-only depending on the layer. If the overhead doubles our effective training budget, we need either better batching or a vLLM fork.
4. **Feature-pack quality is the actual bottleneck**. The hand-selected features in our initial packs may not be the right ones. We provide Delphi-based auto-interp and validator tooling, but discovering the right features for reasoning at scale is its own research project.
5. **Prior art is closer than we think**. Reviewers may decide SARM + CRL + Wilhelm cover 95% of what we're doing. We take the risk upfront by citing them prominently and framing mechreward as a *library + framework*, not as a primary method contribution.

## What this library is NOT

- It's not a claim that mechreward is better than outcome reward or PRM. We don't know yet.
- It's not a safety tool. The hacking-detection framework helps with one axis of robustness, but training a model with mech interp rewards may introduce *new* failure modes we don't yet understand.
- It's not a replacement for SAE training infrastructure. Use `sae_lens` for that.
- It's not a replacement for interpretability research itself. We depend on high-quality SAEs that others train.
